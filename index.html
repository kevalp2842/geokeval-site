Expertise

Search has two layers now. Most brands only see one.

Traditional SEO optimises for ten blue links. But a growing share of search now resolves before a user ever clicks anything — answered directly by AI. I work at the intersection of both: where technical rigour meets the emerging discipline of making brands legible to language models.

01

Technical SEO Foundation

Crawlability, indexation, site architecture, structured data, and Core Web Vitals. The bedrock that everything else depends on — and the most common source of invisible losses.

02

Generative Engine Optimisation

GEO is the practice of making your brand citable, referenceable, and authoritative in the eyes of AI systems. It requires a different set of signals than traditional SEO — and most brands are starting from zero.

03

Content Architecture for AI

Structuring content so that it's not just readable by humans, but parseable by models. This means clear entities, specific claims, and the kind of authoritative depth that LLMs draw from when forming answers.

04

Visibility Measurement

What gets measured, gets managed. I set up monitoring frameworks that track brand presence across both traditional search and generative AI responses — so you're not flying blind.

Why this matters now

The window to act before your competitors do is narrowing.

7+

AI

Generative AI has moved from experiment to primary interface. Perplexity, ChatGPT, Gemini, and Claude now field millions of branded queries daily.

0→1

Most brands have zero GEO strategy. The organisations that move first will establish the citation and authority signals that are hardest to displace later.

I've been tracking the shift from traditional search to generative AI responses since the earliest signs of change. What used to be an academic curiosity is now a commercial reality — AI-generated answers are displacing clicks, reshaping how consumers form opinions, and creating a new kind of invisible brand risk.

The brands that are best positioned in AI answers aren't necessarily the biggest ones. They're the ones that understood early that LLMs have a different definition of authority — and started building for it. That's the work I do.
