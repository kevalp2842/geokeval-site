SEO & GEO Glossary: 45+ Terms Defined

Search now has two overlapping vocabularies — the established language of SEO, and the newer language of generative engine optimisation. This glossary covers both in one place, with short, self-contained definitions you can use and link to.

Why this glossary covers both SEO and GEO

Traditional SEO governs whether your content can be found and ranked by search engines; GEO governs whether it gets retrieved, trusted and cited by generative engines like ChatGPT, Perplexity and Google's AI Overviews. The two aren't rivals — strong SEO foundations (crawlability, indexation, technical health) are the entry ticket GEO builds on top of.

Understanding both vocabularies is now a baseline requirement for anyone working in search. Each entry below is a short, citable definition. Where a term has a full deep-dive elsewhere on this site, it's linked.

A–F G–M N–S T–Z FAQs 45 terms
A– F
AEO (Answer Engine Optimisation) GEO
A term often used interchangeably with GEO, with a slight emphasis on optimising for direct-answer formats — featured snippets, voice assistants, AI answer boxes — rather than generative synthesis specifically. In practice, most practitioners use AEO and GEO to describe the same underlying goal: getting content surfaced and cited in AI-generated answers.
AI Overview GEO
Google's AI-generated summary that appears above traditional organic results for many queries, synthesising information from multiple sources and citing a handful of them. AI Overviews now trigger on a large share of informational queries and carry significantly lower click-through rates than standard results. See why AI is reshaping search.
AI Visibility GEO
An umbrella term for how often, how prominently and how favourably a brand appears across AI-generated answers — measured through metrics like citation frequency and share of model rather than traditional rankings or clicks.
Alt Text SEO
A short text description attached to an image, read by screen readers and search engine crawlers since the image itself can't be parsed as text. Good alt text is accurate and descriptive rather than keyword-stuffed.
Anchor Text SEO
The visible, clickable text of a hyperlink. Descriptive, varied anchor text — rather than repeated exact-match phrases — helps both search engines and AI systems understand what the linked page is about.
Backlink SEO
A link from one website to another. Backlinks have long been a core authority signal in traditional SEO ranking algorithms; in GEO, their influence is weaker and less consistent, with some studies finding AI-cited pages carry fewer backlinks than uncited ones.
Canonical Tag SEO
An HTML tag (rel="canonical") that tells search engines which version of a page is the "master" copy when duplicate or near-duplicate content exists at multiple URLs, consolidating ranking signals onto one preferred version.
Chunking GEO
The process by which an AI system splits a web page into smaller, semantically coherent passages before retrieval. Chunking is why AI citation happens at the passage level rather than the page level — a long page might have just one chunk retrieved and cited. See how AI search engines decide what to cite.
Citation (AI Citation) GEO
A reference an AI engine attaches to a specific claim in its generated answer, pointing to the source it drew that claim from. Earning citations — not rankings — is the primary goal of GEO. See how AI search engines decide what to cite.
Core Web Vitals SEO
Google's set of page-experience metrics measuring loading performance, interactivity and visual stability — currently Largest Contentful Paint (LCP), Interaction to Next Paint (INP), and Cumulative Layout Shift (CLS). A confirmed Google ranking factor and a baseline technical health signal.
Crawl Budget SEO
The number of pages a search engine bot will crawl on a given site within a given timeframe. Crawl budget matters most for very large sites, where inefficient site structure can mean important pages get crawled — and indexed — less frequently.
Dark Discovery GEO
A category of AI-driven brand discovery and influence that occurs with no associated click, referral or analytics trail — visible in business outcomes (brand searches, direct traffic, conversion uplift) but invisible in standard reporting tools such as GA4. Dark discovery is growing as AI answers resolve queries before any click occurs. See generative engine optimisation.
Domain Authority / Domain Rating SEO
Third-party metrics from Moz and Ahrefs respectively that estimate a website's overall ranking strength on a 1–100 scale, based largely on backlink profile. Useful as a directional SEO signal; carries little to no direct weight in AI citation decisions.
E-E-A-T SEO
Google's framework for evaluating content quality: Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness and Trustworthiness. Originally a Google Search Quality Rater guideline, E-E-A-T signals — visible author credentials, real expertise, third-party corroboration — matter for AI citation too, since engines favour authoritative, verifiable sources.
Entity (Entity Authority) GEO
A distinct, identifiable "thing" — a person, brand, product or concept — that search and AI systems can recognise consistently across the web, typically via a knowledge graph. Building entity authority means establishing clear, consistent signals about who or what you are across many independent sources, not just your own site. See GEO.
Featured Snippet SEO
A highlighted answer box shown above standard organic results in Google, pulled directly from a ranking page. Pages that hold a featured snippet have historically shown a notably higher probability of also being cited in AI Overviews — the two formats reward similar structural qualities.
Fluency Optimisation GEO
A content technique tested in the Princeton GEO study (Aggarwal et al., KDD 2024) involving rewriting content in clearer, more readable language. It improved citation visibility across most content categories and performed especially well when combined with adding statistics. See content optimisation for GEO.
G– M
GEO (Generative Engine Optimisation) GEO
The practice of structuring and writing content so that generative AI engines retrieve, trust and cite it in their synthesised answers. Formalised as a research discipline in a 2024 Princeton-led study, GEO is best understood as an evolution of SEO for an answer-first search landscape rather than a replacement for it. See the complete guide to generative engine optimisation.
Grounding GEO
The process by which an AI system anchors a generated claim to a specific retrieved passage, reducing the risk of hallucination by tying the answer to verifiable source material. Grounding is the mechanism that produces citations — without it, a response is pure model generation.
Hallucination GEO
An AI-generated statement presented as fact that isn't supported by any retrieved source — effectively a confident fabrication. Strong grounding and high-quality retrieval reduce hallucination risk, which is one reason engines favour clearly sourced, factually dense content.
Index / Indexation SEO
The process by which a search engine stores and organises a crawled page so it can be returned in results. A page can be crawled but not indexed (for example, if it's low-value or duplicate content) — indexation is the prerequisite for both ranking and AI retrieval.
Information Gain GEO
The degree to which a piece of content adds genuinely new value beyond what's already available elsewhere on a topic, as opposed to restating consensus. AI re-ranking systems tend to favour passages with high information gain — original data, novel analysis, a fresh framework — and structurally devalue repetitive content. See content optimisation for GEO.
Internal Linking SEO
Hyperlinks connecting pages within the same website. A deliberate internal linking structure — such as a pillar-and-cluster model — distributes topical relevance and authority across a site and helps both search crawlers and AI retrieval systems understand how content relates.
Keyword Density SEO
The frequency of a target keyword relative to total word count on a page — a once-central SEO metric. Keyword density has little to no direct influence on AI retrieval, since generative engines match content by semantic meaning rather than exact wording; over-optimising for density can actively hurt citation chances.
Knowledge Graph GEO
A structured database of entities and the relationships between them, used by Google and AI systems to understand "things" rather than just strings of text. A well-established entity in a knowledge graph is more reliably retrieved and disambiguated by AI engines.
LLMO (Large Language Model Optimisation) GEO
A term sometimes used alongside GEO and AEO, with a slight emphasis on optimising for how large language models represent and recall information about a brand or topic — including content baked into the model's training data, not only live retrieval.
llms.txt GEO
A proposed plain-text file, placed at a website's root, that gives AI crawlers a curated, structured summary of a site's most important content — intended as an AI-era counterpart to robots.txt. Adoption and support vary by platform, but it's a low-cost addition worth including as part of technical GEO.
Meta Description SEO
The short summary text shown beneath a page's title in search results, written in HTML but not a direct ranking factor — its job is to earn the click, and increasingly, to give engines a clean, accurate summary of the page's purpose.
N– S
Off-Page SEO SEO
SEO activity that happens away from your own website — backlink building, digital PR, brand mentions and other signals that build external authority and corroboration. Off-page signals matter for GEO too, since AI engines weigh corroboration across independent domains, including unlinked brand mentions.
On-Page SEO SEO
SEO activity applied directly to a page — content quality, title tags, headings, internal links and technical structure. The foundation that both classic ranking and AI retrieval are built on.
PageRank SEO
Google's original link-analysis algorithm, which scored pages based on the quantity and quality of links pointing to them. Still part of Google's broader ranking system in evolved form, though far less influential on its own than in Google's early years — and largely irrelevant to AI citation decisions.
Passage Retrieval GEO
The process by which an AI system identifies and pulls the specific text passages most relevant to a query, rather than evaluating a whole page. Passage-level retrieval is the central architectural shift that separates GEO from traditional SEO — your content is competed at the paragraph level, not the page level. See how AI search engines decide what to cite.
Query Fan-Out GEO
The process by which a generative engine breaks one user question into multiple parallel sub-queries to retrieve a fuller set of relevant sources, then synthesises an answer from across them. A large share of AI citations are attributed to these fan-out sub-queries rather than the original head term. See how AI search engines decide what to cite.
RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) GEO
The architecture underpinning most generative search engines: the system retrieves relevant passages from external content first, then feeds them to a language model alongside the original question to generate a grounded, cited answer. RAG is why citation happens at the passage level and why content structure matters so much for GEO.
Re-Ranking GEO
The filtering stage in a RAG pipeline where retrieved candidate passages are scored on signals like relevance, factual density, authority, freshness and structural clarity — and most are discarded before the final answer is generated. Surviving re-ranking, not just being retrieved, is what determines citation. See how AI search engines decide what to cite.
Robots.txt SEO
A plain-text file at a website's root that instructs crawlers — including AI bots — which parts of a site they may or may not access. Misconfigured robots.txt rules, including default settings on some hosting platforms, can silently block AI crawlers from a site entirely.
Schema Markup (Structured Data) SEO
Code added to a page — typically in JSON-LD format, using the Schema.org vocabulary — that explicitly labels what different pieces of content are (an article, a FAQ, a product, an author), helping both search engines and AI systems parse and trust the content with less ambiguity.
Semantic Search SEO
Search based on the meaning of a query rather than exact keyword matching, using vector embeddings to find conceptually related content even when the wording differs. Semantic search is the foundation that both modern Google ranking and all generative engine retrieval are built on.
SERP (Search Engine Results Page) SEO
The page returned after a search query, traditionally a ranked list of links, now increasingly populated with AI Overviews, featured snippets and other non-link features that reduce the visual and functional space available to standard organic results.
Share of Model / Share of Voice GEO
A GEO measurement metric describing what percentage of relevant AI-generated answers in a category mention or cite your brand, relative to competitors — the generative-search equivalent of share of voice in traditional marketing. Tracking share of model is how GEO performance is measured at scale.
Sitemap (XML) SEO
A file listing a website's important URLs, submitted to search engines to help them discover and crawl content more efficiently — particularly useful for large or frequently updated sites.
T– Z
Title Tag SEO
The HTML element defining a page's title, shown as the clickable headline in search results and browser tabs. One of the strongest on-page SEO signals, and a strong contributor to heading–query alignment in AI retrieval too.
Topical Authority GEO
The degree to which a website is recognised — by both search engines and AI systems — as a comprehensive, trustworthy source on a given subject, typically built through a structured cluster of interlinked content covering a topic in depth rather than isolated pages.
Vector Embedding GEO
A numerical representation of a piece of text's meaning, positioned in a high-dimensional space such that semantically similar content sits close together. Embeddings are what allow AI retrieval systems to match a query to relevant content even when no exact keywords overlap — the mathematical foundation of semantic search.
Zero-Click Search GEO
A search session that ends without the user clicking through to any external website, because their answer was resolved directly on the results page via an AI Overview, featured snippet or similar feature. Zero-click behaviour is significantly higher on AI-powered surfaces than on traditional search. See why AI is reshaping search.

Common questions

Frequently asked questions

  • SEO optimises content to rank in a list of links; GEO optimises content to be retrieved and cited inside a synthesised AI answer. SEO foundations — crawlability, indexation, technical health — remain the entry ticket GEO builds on top of. The two disciplines overlap significantly: strong SEO structure almost always aids GEO performance too.
  • Largely, yes. Most practitioners use GEO and AEO to describe the same goal — visibility inside AI-generated answers — with AEO carrying a slightly stronger historical link to direct-answer formats like featured snippets and voice search. LLMO is a third term with slightly different emphasis (training data vs. live retrieval). In practice, all three describe adjacent parts of the same problem.
  • Partly. Many GEO concepts (RAG, query fan-out, chunking, grounding) describe genuinely new mechanics, but they sit on top of SEO fundamentals you likely already know. This glossary is designed to bridge the two rather than treat them as separate worlds — if you understand traditional SEO, most GEO concepts will click quickly.
  • Start with RAG, query fan-out, citation and passage retrieval — together they explain the core mechanic everything else builds on. Once you understand how generative engines retrieve and rank passages before generating an answer, the content optimisation implications follow naturally. The how AI search engines decide what to cite guide walks through how they fit together.
