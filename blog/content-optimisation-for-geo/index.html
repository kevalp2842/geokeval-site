Content Optimisation for GEO: The Levers That Earn Citations

A Princeton study on generative engine optimisation found that the right on-page changes lift AI citation rates by up to 40%. Most of those gains come from six specific levers — none of which require a full rewrite. This guide shows you exactly what they are, with before-and-after examples for each.

40% Max visibility lift from GEO content changes Aggarwal et al., Princeton / KDD 2024
+115% Citation lift for rank-5 pages using proper source citation Search Engine Land, 2024
4.4× Higher conversion rate from AI-referred visitors Semrush, 2025

Why AI reads content differently

When a user types a query into ChatGPT, Perplexity or Google AI Overviews, the system does not rank pages by domain authority and serve a list of links. It retrieves candidate passages, scores them for relevance and trustworthiness, and assembles a synthesised answer — with citations pointing to the sources it found most useful.

That passage-retrieval model changes what "good content" means. The AI is not rewarding the page that appears most authoritative in the abstract; it is selecting the passage that most directly and credibly answers the exact question. Content that buries its point, hedges its claims, or relies on implied expertise rather than named sources is systematically passed over — regardless of how well it ranks in traditional search.

The six levers below are the on-page changes most consistently shown to flip that outcome.

Lever 1 — Lead every section with a direct answer

AI retrieval systems select passages that answer the query with minimal inference. If your answer is buried three paragraphs into a section — after context, caveats, and background — the retriever will often select a competitor's passage that leads with the answer instead.

The fix is simple: write the conclusion of each section first, then explain it. This is the journalistic "inverted pyramid" applied to every H2 and H3 on the page.

Before

AI Overviews have been rolling out across different markets at varying speeds, and Google has been testing different formats. There are several factors that determine whether a page appears. At a high level, it comes down to content quality and how well the page answers the user's query.

After

To appear in Google AI Overviews, a page needs to answer the query directly, use structured headings, and cite credible sources. Google selects passages that require the least inference to be useful — so content that leads with the answer outperforms content that builds up to it.

Apply this to every section of every page you want cited. If your H2 asks a question, the first sentence below it should answer that question directly.

Lever 2 — Add verifiable statistics

The Princeton GEO study found that adding cited statistics was one of the two highest-impact individual changes you can make to content. The reason: AI systems are trained to prefer factual, verifiable claims over general assertions, and a statistic with a named source provides exactly that — a retrievable, checkable fact.

Vague language ("many businesses", "significant growth", "studies suggest") carries no epistemic weight in an AI retrieval context. A specific number from a named study does.

Before

AI search is growing rapidly and many users are turning to tools like ChatGPT instead of traditional search engines. This is having a significant impact on organic traffic for websites across many industries.

After

ChatGPT reached 400 million weekly active users by February 2025 (OpenAI). A Semrush study of 50,000 domains found AI-referred visitors convert at 4.4× the rate of organic search visitors — making citations commercially significant even at lower traffic volumes.

Aim for at least two data points per major section, each attributed to a specific source. Where possible, use the format: [Statistic] ([Source], [Year]). This mirrors the citation format AI systems use themselves.

Lever 3 — Incorporate expert quotations

Named expert quotations are the second highest-impact lever from the Princeton research. They provide two signals simultaneously: attributed authority (a specific person with expertise said this) and factual specificity (a direct statement, not a paraphrase).

The expert does not need to be world-famous. A named practitioner, researcher, or executive from a credible organisation is sufficient. What matters is that the quotation is clearly attributed, not anonymous.

Before

Experts agree that the shift to AI search requires brands to think differently about how they create content. The focus needs to be on providing clear, direct answers rather than optimising purely for rankings.

After

"The brands that will win in AI search are the ones that become genuinely useful to AI systems — not the ones that game them," says Keval Patel, GEO strategist at geokeval. "That means writing for comprehension, not keyword density."

One strong quotation per article is enough to move the needle. Three or more quotations from different named sources is ideal for any guide or explainer piece.

Lever 4 — Cite authoritative sources

Citing your sources in the body of your content — not just in a footnote — signals trustworthiness to AI retrieval systems. A Search Engine Land analysis found that pages previously ranked fifth which added proper source citations gained an average of 115% more AI citations.

The mechanism is straightforward: AI models are trained on human-written content that cites sources when making factual claims. Content that follows this convention is scored as more credible. Content that doesn't appears to be making unverified assertions.

Before

Zero-click searches now account for the majority of Google queries. This means that most users get their answer from the search results page itself without clicking through to any website.

After

Zero-click searches now account for approximately 60% of all Google queries (SparkToro / Datos, 2024). With Google AI Overviews active, that figure rises to 80–83%, and in AI Mode reaches an estimated 93% (Semrush, 2025).

Prioritise citing: academic research, government datasets, industry analyst reports (Semrush, SparkToro, Gartner), and major media outlets. Citing your own content is fine for internal linking but does not provide the same trust signal as external authorities.

Lever 5 — Write prompt-aligned FAQ sections

FAQ sections earn AI citations at a disproportionate rate because they pre-format content as question-and-answer pairs — exactly the structure AI retrieval looks for. A well-written FAQ also targets the long-tail conversational queries that are most common in AI search interfaces.

The key is to write FAQs around the actual questions users ask, not the questions you wish they'd ask. Use search autocomplete, Reddit threads, and your own search console data to identify real phrasing.

Before

Q: What is GEO?
A: GEO stands for Generative Engine Optimisation. It is the practice of optimising content for AI search engines. It is becoming increasingly important as AI search grows.

After

Q: What is generative engine optimisation (GEO)?
A: Generative engine optimisation (GEO) is the practice of making content more likely to be cited by AI answer engines such as ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google AI Overviews. Unlike traditional SEO — which targets ranked blue links — GEO targets the passage-retrieval and citation mechanisms that power AI-generated answers.

Each FAQ answer should be self-contained: assume the reader arrives at only that question-and-answer, with no prior context. Answers of 40–120 words perform best — long enough to be substantive, short enough to be retrieved as a complete passage.

Lever 6 — Structure for extraction

AI systems extract answers from HTML. Structurally ambiguous content — long unbroken paragraphs, mixed topics within a single section, key facts buried in the middle of a block of prose — is harder to extract cleanly. Content that is easy to extract earns more citations.

Practical structural improvements:

  • Use descriptive H2 and H3 headings that tell the AI exactly what the section covers — not clever headings that require context to interpret.
  • Put key definitions in their own paragraph, ideally as the first sentence under the relevant heading.
  • Use bullet lists or numbered steps for anything that is naturally enumerable. Prose-format lists are consistently under-extracted.
  • Use tables for comparisons, timelines, and feature matrices — AI systems handle tabular data well.
  • Keep paragraph length under 80 words for informational content. Long paragraphs are more likely to be truncated in extraction.
Before

When it comes to AI search visibility there are a number of different factors to consider including the structure of your content, whether you are citing sources, and also how directly you answer the question being asked by the user, which can vary a lot depending on the platform.

After

Three factors most influence AI citation rates: (1) answer-first structure — does the section lead with the answer? (2) source citations — are factual claims attributed to named sources? (3) extraction clarity — is the key information in a clearly bounded paragraph, list, or table?

What actively hurts your citation chances

Beyond applying the six levers, it is worth removing content patterns that AI retrieval systems consistently deprioritise:

  • Introductory padding. Paragraphs that restate the question, explain why it matters, and promise to answer it — before actually answering it. Cut to the answer immediately.
  • Excessive hedging. "It depends," "there are many factors," and "it can vary" without specifics provide no extractable information. Qualify if necessary, but always follow with a concrete answer.
  • Promotional language. Claims that your product or service is "industry-leading," "best-in-class," or "unique" without evidence read as marketing, not information. AI systems deprioritise them.
  • Thin FAQ answers. Single-sentence answers to complex questions signal low effort. AI systems strongly prefer FAQ answers that are substantive (at least 40 words).
  • Duplicate content across pages. If multiple pages on your site say the same thing, AI systems may pick only one — or none — as each dilutes the signal of the others.
  • Keyword-stuffed headings. Headings like "Best GEO SEO AI Search Optimisation Tips 2026" confuse rather than clarify. Descriptive, natural headings outperform keyword-dense ones in retrieval.

The GEO content checklist

Before publishing or updating any piece of content you want AI systems to cite, check it against these six criteria:

  • Every major section (H2/H3) opens with a direct, specific answer to the question implied by that heading
  • At least two verifiable statistics appear in the article, each attributed to a named source with year
  • At least one named expert quotation appears, clearly attributed
  • Factual claims cite external authoritative sources (academic, government, or major industry publications)
  • A structured FAQ section covers the most common conversational queries on the topic, with answers of 40–120 words each
  • Key information is in clearly bounded paragraphs, numbered lists, or tables — not buried in long prose blocks

Pages that satisfy all six criteria consistently outperform those that satisfy three or fewer. If a page fails three or more checks, prioritise it for optimisation before any other on-page work.

These levers apply across all the major AI answer engines — ChatGPT, Perplexity, Google AI Overviews, and Copilot — because they all use the same underlying passage-retrieval architecture. Optimising for one optimises for all.
