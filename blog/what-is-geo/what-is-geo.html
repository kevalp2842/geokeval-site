What is Generative Engine Optimisation (GEO)?

AI tools like ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Gemini now answer millions of search queries directly — without the user clicking a single link. GEO is the discipline of making your brand visible, citable, and authoritative in those answers.

What is Generative Engine Optimisation?

Generative Engine Optimisation — GEO — is the practice of optimising a brand's digital presence so that it is visible, accurately represented, and cited by AI-powered search tools.

These tools include ChatGPT (with Browse), Perplexity, Google's AI Overviews, Gemini, Claude, and a growing number of AI-native search interfaces. What they have in common is that they generate answers to queries directly, drawing on their training data and live web retrieval — rather than simply returning a list of links.

When a user asks ChatGPT "What's the best project management tool for remote teams?" or "Which skincare brands do dermatologists recommend?", the model constructs an answer. It may cite sources, it may not. But it will include some brands and exclude others. That inclusion or exclusion is not random — it's driven by signals. GEO is the work of understanding and optimising for those signals.

"GEO is not a replacement for SEO. It is the next layer — built on top of a solid technical and content foundation, but requiring an entirely different set of signals."

How AI search engines work

To understand GEO, you first need a basic model of how generative AI tools produce answers about brands and topics.

Training data

Large language models are trained on vast corpora of text — web pages, books, articles, forums, documentation. During this training, the model develops associations between entities (brands, people, products, concepts) and the language used to describe them. A brand that appears frequently, consistently, and authoritatively across a wide range of credible sources develops stronger representation in the model's weights.

Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG)

Many AI search tools now combine model knowledge with live web retrieval. When you ask Perplexity a question, it searches the web in real time, pulls relevant pages, and synthesises an answer. The quality and structure of your web content directly affects whether your pages are retrieved and whether the model draws from them.

Entity recognition and knowledge graphs

AI models don't just read text — they build internal representations of entities and their relationships. They understand that a brand is a type of organisation, that it operates in certain sectors, that it has certain attributes. The clearer and more consistent these signals are across the web, the more confidently a model can include that brand in a relevant answer.

Key point

AI models form opinions about brands the same way humans do — by encountering consistent, authoritative information across multiple trusted sources over time. The difference is that once formed, those model weights are hard to shift. Getting the signals right early matters disproportionately.

GEO vs traditional SEO — what's different

Traditional SEO and GEO share a foundation — you cannot do GEO well on a technically broken or content-thin site. But the signals that drive visibility in AI-generated answers are meaningfully different from those that drive rankings in a traditional SERP.

Dimension Traditional SEO Generative Engine Optimisation
Primary goal Rank on page one for target keywords Be cited in AI-generated answers
Key signals Backlinks, on-page optimisation, Core Web Vitals Entity authority, content depth, citation patterns
Content format Keyword-optimised pages, structured headers Clear claims, specific data, citable prose
Measurement Rankings, organic traffic, CTR Brand presence in AI responses, citation frequency
Timeline 3–6 months to see meaningful movement Varies; model retraining cycles affect outcomes
Maturity 25+ years of established practice Emerging — best practices still forming

The most important distinction is this: traditional SEO optimises for an algorithm that scores pages against a query. GEO optimises for a model that has formed a view of your brand — and is drawing on that view to construct an answer. These are fundamentally different mechanisms.

Why GEO matters now

You might reasonably ask: AI search is still a small share of total search volume. Why invest in it now?

Three reasons.

  • The shift is faster than most people think. Google's AI Overviews now appear on a significant proportion of searches. Perplexity's growth has been steep. ChatGPT is embedded in consumer behaviour in a way that would have seemed implausible two years ago.
  • First-mover advantage compounds. Brands that establish strong entity signals and citation patterns early will be harder to displace when the market catches up. Authority in AI models, like domain authority in traditional SEO, takes time to build.
  • Most brands are starting from zero. GEO is still early enough that the competitive bar is low. Acting now means building an advantage before competitors even understand the problem.

The four pillars of GEO

Based on what we currently understand about how AI models form and retrieve information about brands, effective GEO rests on four interconnected areas.

1. Technical foundation

Clean crawlability, proper indexation, structured data markup, and a coherent site architecture are prerequisites. AI retrieval systems respect the same technical signals as traditional search crawlers — if your site is blocking pages inadvertently, missing schema markup, or serving thin content, no amount of GEO strategy will compensate.

2. Entity authority

Your brand needs to be clearly defined as an entity across the web — Wikipedia entries, industry directories, press coverage, social profiles, and your own website all contribute to the model's understanding of what your brand is, what it does, and who it serves. Consistency of name, description, and category signals across these sources is foundational.

3. Citable content

Content that gets cited by AI tools tends to share certain characteristics: it makes specific, verifiable claims; it uses clear, declarative prose; it addresses questions directly; and it carries signals of expertise (author credentials, sources, data). This is different from keyword-optimised content, which can be vaguer and more formulaic.

4. Measurement and iteration

You cannot improve what you cannot measure. GEO measurement involves systematically prompting AI tools with relevant queries and tracking whether and how your brand appears — using tools like Peec AI, Otterly, and custom prompt frameworks. This is still an emerging discipline, but the core principle is straightforward: build a baseline, implement changes, measure the delta.

Getting started with GEO

If you want to understand where your brand currently stands in AI-generated search, the simplest starting point is manual: open ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Gemini, and ask the questions your target audience would ask. Does your brand appear? How is it described? What competitors appear alongside you, or instead of you?

What you find will tell you a great deal about the gap between where you are and where you want to be — and give you a starting point for a structured GEO audit.

Try it now

Ask an AI tool: "What are the leading [your category] brands in the UK?" or "Which [your product type] do experts recommend?" If your brand doesn't appear, or appears inaccurately, that's the gap GEO is designed to close.

Final thoughts

GEO is not a passing trend. It is the natural extension of search optimisation into a world where the search interface is increasingly a conversation rather than a query — where the answer is generated, not retrieved.

The brands that understand this early, and invest in building the right signals, will have a structural advantage that compounds over time. The brands that wait will find themselves trying to catch up in a landscape where the patterns are already set.
