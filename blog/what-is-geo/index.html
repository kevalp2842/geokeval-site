What is Generative Engine Optimisation (GEO)?

AI tools now answer millions of search queries every day — without users clicking a single link. GEO is the discipline of making your brand visible, citable, and authoritative in those answers. Here's what it is, how it works, and why it matters now.

The search landscape has quietly changed

For the past two decades, SEO has meant one thing: ranking on Google. Get your pages to appear in the top ten results, earn the click, drive the traffic. The model was simple, measurable, and well understood.

That model is no longer the whole picture. A growing share of search queries are now being answered directly by AI — by ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, Claude, and others. Users ask a question and get a synthesised answer, often without ever visiting a website. No blue links. No clicks. Just a response.

For brands, this creates a new and urgent problem: if your brand isn't in the AI's answer, you effectively don't exist for that query.

"In traditional search, you compete for position. In AI search, you compete to be cited at all. Those are fundamentally different challenges."

What is Generative Engine Optimisation?

Generative Engine Optimisation — GEO — is the practice of making your brand visible, credible, and citable within AI-generated search responses. It's the answer to the question: how do you show up when there are no rankings to rank for?

Unlike traditional SEO, which focuses on signals like backlinks, page speed, and keyword placement, GEO focuses on a different set of inputs:

  • Entity clarity — does the AI understand who and what your brand is?
  • Authoritative depth — does your content demonstrate genuine expertise on a topic?
  • Citability — is your content structured in a way that's easy for a model to reference?
  • Corroboration — does the wider web reinforce what you say about yourself?

How AI systems decide what to surface

Large language models like GPT-4, Gemini, and Claude are trained on vast amounts of web content. When a user asks a question, the model draws on that training — plus, in some cases, real-time retrieval — to compose an answer.

The brands that appear in those answers are not necessarily the biggest or best-known. They are the ones that the model has encountered frequently, in credible contexts, with consistent and specific information attached to them.

The role of entity authority

AI models build internal representations of entities — people, companies, products, concepts. The richer and more consistent that representation, the more likely the model is to reference it when composing a relevant answer. Sparse, inconsistent, or contradictory information about your brand weakens your entity authority and reduces your chances of being cited.

The role of content structure

Models prefer content that makes clear, specific, attributable claims. Vague brand copy — the kind optimised for persuasion rather than information — performs poorly in AI contexts. Content written with factual precision, clear structure, and specific evidence tends to be drawn on more heavily.

How GEO differs from traditional SEO

It's important to be clear: GEO does not replace SEO. The technical foundations — crawlability, indexation, structured data, site architecture — remain essential. Without them, your content may not reach the training data or retrieval systems that AI models rely on.

But GEO adds a layer on top. Where SEO asks "can search engines find and understand my content?", GEO asks "will AI systems reference my brand when forming an answer?". The optimisation targets are different, the success metrics are different, and the content strategy is different.

The four pillars of a GEO strategy

1. Technical foundation

Ensure your site is technically sound. Clean architecture, fast load times, structured data markup, and accurate indexation are prerequisites. If a model's retrieval system can't access your content, nothing else matters.

2. Entity building

Develop a consistent, well-corroborated entity presence across the web. This means Wikipedia entries where appropriate, consistent NAP data, authoritative mentions in third-party publications, and a clear brand narrative that appears across multiple credible sources.

3. Content for AI legibility

Audit your content for specificity and citability. Replace vague claims with measurable ones. Structure content with clear headings, definitions, and factual statements. Anticipate the questions your audience asks AI tools and create content that directly answers them.

4. Measurement

Track your brand's presence in AI-generated answers. Tools like Peec AI, Otterly, and custom prompt-monitoring frameworks allow you to test how often and how accurately your brand appears across different AI platforms. What gets measured, gets managed.

Why now is the moment to act

GEO is still an emerging discipline. Most brands have no strategy at all. The organisations that move now — that start building entity authority, auditing their content for AI legibility, and establishing measurement frameworks — will have a significant advantage over those that wait.

AI-generated answers, unlike search rankings, tend to be sticky. The citations and brand references that models form early are harder to displace once established. This isn't a space where you can afford to play catch-up.

Most brands have zero GEO strategy. The organisations that move first will establish the citation and authority signals that are hardest to displace later.

Where to start

If you're new to GEO, the most valuable first step is an honest audit of where your brand currently stands in AI responses. Ask the tools your customers use the questions they're likely to ask. See if your brand appears, and if so, how accurately it's represented.

From there, the work is methodical: strengthen your technical foundation, build your entity presence, restructure your content for AI legibility, and put measurement in place. None of it is magic — but all of it requires deliberate effort.

That's what GEO is. And it's the most consequential shift in search since mobile.

Each has different retrieval behaviours — Perplexity performs real-time web searches, while ChatGPT draws primarily on training data with optional browsing — so a well-rounded GEO strategy accounts for all of them.
  • AI models build internal representations of entities — people, companies, products, and concepts — based on the content they are trained on and, in retrieval-augmented systems, what they find via web search. Brands that appear frequently, consistently, and in credible contexts across multiple sources are more likely to be cited. Sparse, contradictory, or vague brand information weakens a model's entity representation and reduces citation likelihood.
  • No. GEO and SEO are complementary disciplines, not substitutes. The technical foundations of SEO — crawlability, indexation, site architecture, structured data — remain prerequisites for GEO, since AI retrieval systems depend on the same infrastructure. GEO adds a layer on top: once your content is technically accessible, GEO work ensures it is cited by AI systems rather than just ranked by search engines.
  • The most direct method is manual prompt auditing: run the questions your customers are likely to ask across ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, and Claude, and record whether your brand appears, in what position, and how it is described. Dedicated GEO monitoring tools automate this at scale. On the analytics side, you can segment GA4 to isolate known AI referrer domains, though a significant portion of AI-driven traffic arrives as "Direct" with no referrer.
  • An llms.txt file is an emerging convention — similar in concept to robots.txt — that provides AI systems with a structured, plain-text summary of your site's content and key information. It is designed to make it easier for AI crawlers to understand your brand without parsing full HTML pages. It is not yet universally adopted or required, but adding one is a low-effort signal that forward-looking AI systems may use.
  • GEO timelines vary significantly by platform and tactic. Changes visible to real-time retrieval systems like Perplexity can take effect within days of content being indexed. Influencing a model's training data takes much longer — months to years, depending on training cycles. Entity-building work (earning consistent, credible mentions across the web) is a long-term investment. Most brands should expect meaningful movement over a 3–6 month window of sustained effort.
  • The most impactful content changes for GEO are: replacing vague brand copy with specific, factual, attributable claims; adding clear definitions and structured headings that directly answer likely questions; including measurable evidence and third-party corroboration; and writing content that anticipates the exact questions your audience asks AI tools. Persuasion-optimised copy performs poorly in AI contexts — precision and specificity are what get cited.
  • In GA4, create a custom channel group under Admin → Data display → Channel groups, with rules matching Session source to known AI referrer domains: chat.openai.com, chatgpt.com, perplexity.ai, gemini.google.com, copilot.microsoft.com, and claude.ai. This captures the visible referral traffic. A significant portion of AI-driven visits arrives as "Direct" with no referrer — particularly from mobile apps — so also monitor landing page patterns: unexplained spikes in direct traffic to specific answer-style pages are a strong secondary signal.
